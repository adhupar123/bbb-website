Project Overview

The goal of this project is to analyze data regarding current drugs and their ability to cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB), with the eventual aim of creating an equation based on the analyzed data to generate a predictive model. This model is based on drugs which have the ability to cross the Blood-Brain Barrier and their chemical and physical properties.

The information analyzed is used to generate a mathematical equation which can graphically determine a drug's ability to cross the BBB based on chosen molecular descriptors.

Primary Objective: Develop a predictive model to determine whether a drug can cross the blood-brain barrier based on its molecular properties.

Secondary Objectives: Identify key molecular descriptors that influence BBB permeability and provide insights for drug design targeting central nervous system disorders.

Background Information

What is the Blood-Brain Barrier?

The blood-brain barrier (BBB) is a highly selective semipermeable border of endothelial cells that prevents solutes in the circulating blood from non-selectively crossing into the extracellular fluid of the central nervous system where neurons reside. Understanding BBB permeability is crucial for developing drugs that treat neurological disorders such as Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, brain tumors, and epilepsy.

LogBB: The Key Metric

LogBB describes the concentration of a drug found on either side of the BBB. It is calculated as the logarithm of the ratio of drug concentration in the brain to drug concentration in the blood:

Where:

  • LogBB > 0: Higher concentration in brain than blood (good BBB penetration)
  • LogBB = 0: Equal concentration in brain and blood
  • LogBB < 0: Lower concentration in brain than blood (poor BBB penetration)

Molecular Descriptors

We analyzed six key molecular descriptors that influence a drug's ability to cross the BBB. These descriptors were extracted using the RDKit package based on each molecule's SMILES formula (a computer-friendly chemical notation).

üíß Lipophilicity (MolLogP)

Measures a compound's fat solubility. Higher lipophilicity generally improves membrane permeability, as the BBB is composed of lipid-rich cell membranes. Correlation with LogBB: +0.28

‚öñÔ∏è Molecular Weight (MolWt)

The mass of the molecule. Larger molecules have difficulty diffusing across biological membranes. Generally, drugs under 500 Da have better BBB penetration. Correlation with LogBB: -0.21

üéØ Topological Polar Surface Area (TPSA)

Measures the surface area occupied by polar atoms. Lower TPSA values (<90 ≈≤) are preferred for BBB penetration as they indicate less polarity. Strongest correlation with LogBB: -0.53

‚ö° Formal Charge (FC)

The net electrical charge on the molecule. Charged molecules have reduced membrane crossing ability due to the lipophilic nature of cell membranes. Correlation with LogBB: ~0.00

üíç Aromatic Rings

The number of aromatic ring structures in the molecule. While aromatic rings can improve stability, too many can increase molecular weight and reduce flexibility. Correlation with LogBB: -0.10

üî¨ Heavy Atoms

Non-hydrogen atoms in the molecule. Related to molecular weight, this descriptor influences the overall size and complexity of the drug molecule. Correlation with LogBB: -0.21

Dataset

The dataset used in this project was sourced from the Blood-Brain Barrier Database (B3DB) on GitHub. It includes data on approximately 7,000 drugs and their ability to cross the BBB.

Our analysis focused on a subset of approximately 1,000 drugs that have experimentally determined LogBB values, allowing us to build and validate predictive models with ground truth data.

Data Source: F. Meng, Y. Xi, J. Huang, and P. W. Ayers, "A curated diverse molecular database of blood-brain barrier permeability with chemical descriptors," Scientific Data, vol. 8, no. 1, p. 289, Oct. 2021, doi: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41597-021-01069-5.

Methodology

üìä Analysis Pipeline

Our computational approach consisted of several stages:

  • Data Preparation: Cleaned the dataset and extracted molecular descriptors using RDKit
  • Exploratory Analysis: Examined distributions and correlations between descriptors and LogBB
  • Model Development: Built and compared multiple regression models
  • Validation: Evaluated model performance using R¬≤ scores and prediction accuracy
  • Simulation: Applied Fick's Law to model drug diffusion across the BBB

Models Developed

Multiple Linear Regression

Combined all descriptors into a single model to predict LogBB values. Achieved R¬≤ = 0.333, explaining 33.3% of variance in BBB permeability.

Logistic Regression

Binary classification model to predict whether a drug crosses the BBB (LogBB > 0). Achieved 70.2% accuracy on test data.

Gradient Boosting

Advanced machine learning model using ensemble learning. Best performing model with R¬≤ = 0.357 and MAE = 0.437.

Key Findings

üîç Most Important Factors

TPSA showed the strongest correlation with BBB permeability (-0.53), indicating that polar surface area is the most critical factor. Drugs with TPSA < 90 ≈≤ generally show better BBB penetration.

Lipophilicity (MolLogP) showed a positive correlation (+0.28), confirming that more lipophilic drugs cross the BBB more readily, though excessive lipophilicity can cause other issues.

Aromatic Rings had a negative coefficient (-0.13), suggesting that reducing aromatic ring count can improve BBB permeability while maintaining drug efficacy.

Implications for Drug Design

Our findings suggest that for improved BBB penetration, drug candidates should be designed with:

  • Lower topological polar surface area (TPSA < 90 ≈≤)
  • Moderate lipophilicity (MolLogP between 1-3)
  • Molecular weight under 500 Da
  • Minimal formal charge (neutral molecules preferred)
  • Reduced number of aromatic rings
